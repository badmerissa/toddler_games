App stores are full of games marketed at young children. Many are genuinely good; many more are designed to extract attention, money, or data. As a parent, it can be hard to tell the difference quickly. This checklist is designed to help.

## The five questions to ask before downloading

### 1. Does it require an account or personal information?

Any app that asks for your child's name, date of birth, email address, or location before they can play is collecting data about your child. For toddlers, this is unnecessary. Good toddler apps require nothing beyond opening them.

**What to look for:** Apps that work immediately on opening, with no sign-up screen.

### 2. Does it show advertising?

Advertising directed at children under five is ethically questionable and, in many jurisdictions, legally restricted. Banner ads, interstitial ads, and "reward" ads (watch a video to continue) are particularly harmful for young children, who cannot distinguish advertising from content.

**What to look for:** A one-time paid app, or a clearly free ad-free app with transparent funding.

### 3. Does it have in-app purchases or paywalls?

Apps with in-app purchases often use psychological techniques — urgency, artificial scarcity, the frustration of locked content — to prompt purchases. Young children cannot understand the concept of spending real money.

**What to look for:** Apps with no in-app purchases at all, or where all content is available without payment.

### 4. What data does it collect, and where does it go?

Under GDPR (UK/EU) and COPPA (US), apps are required to disclose what data they collect from children. Check the privacy policy — if it is absent, vague, or mentions sharing data with "partners", treat this as a red flag.

**What to look for:** Apps that explicitly state they collect no personal data, or that all data is stored locally on the device only.

### 5. Is the activity clearly defined, or does it run forever?

Apps designed around endless scrolling, autoplay, or addictive loop mechanics are not designed for toddlers — they are designed for engagement at any cost. Toddlers benefit from activities with a natural endpoint.

**What to look for:** Apps with discrete activities (a colouring page to complete, a puzzle to finish) rather than infinite feeds or streams.

## A quick reference checklist

- [ ] No account or personal information required
- [ ] No advertising of any kind
- [ ] No in-app purchases
- [ ] Clear, honest privacy policy (or explicit statement that no data is collected)
- [ ] Activities with a defined start and end
- [ ] Age-appropriate content and difficulty
- [ ] Works offline (no dependency on a network connection for basic play)
- [ ] Designed to be used with parent involvement, not to replace it

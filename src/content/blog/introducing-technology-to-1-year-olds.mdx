There is no single right age to introduce a child to technology. Context, temperament, family habits, and the type of technology all matter more than a specific milestone age. This guide offers practical, age-by-age signposts based on current developmental research.

## Under 12 months

At this stage, babies are developing their senses and beginning to understand object permanence. Screens offer very little that serves this development. The NHS, WHO, and AAP all recommend avoiding screen media for children under one, with the exception of video calls with family members — which involve real human interaction, turn-taking, and emotional responsiveness.

**Practical note:** If you use a phone or tablet yourself around your baby, they will notice and be curious. This is normal. Turning the screen away occasionally helps establish that screens are not always the focus of attention.

## 12–18 months

Toddlers at this age are beginning symbolic play — understanding that a toy banana represents a real banana, that a drawing can represent an object. However, transfer of learning from a screen to the real world (called "video deficit") is still significant at this age. Children under 18 months generally learn poorly from video; they learn well from direct physical interaction and real-world play.

**If you do introduce screens:** Keep it to short, interactive sessions (under 10 minutes). Video calls with grandparents are genuinely beneficial. Avoid passive video content.

## 18–24 months

Around 18–24 months, the video deficit begins to reduce. Children can start to learn from high-quality interactive media if a parent or caregiver watches and engages alongside them. This is the earliest age at which some interactive apps — simple cause-and-effect games, naming games, colouring — may offer genuine developmental benefit.

**Key principle:** Co-engagement. If you are not present and engaged, the value is minimal.

## 2–3 years

By age two, many children can successfully interact with touchscreens. Fine motor control has developed enough to tap accurately. Language is developing rapidly, and well-designed apps can support vocabulary, colour naming, and shape recognition.

This is an appropriate age to introduce simple creative apps — colouring, building, simple puzzles — for short, defined sessions alongside physical play.

**Suggested session length:** 10–20 minutes, with a clear natural end point (completing a colouring page, finishing a puzzle).

## 3–5 years

Children at this age can engage with slightly more complex interactive content. They can follow simple instructions, understand cause and effect more deeply, and begin to narrate their own play. Apps that support storytelling, creativity, and problem-solving are most valuable.

At this age, it is also appropriate to begin having conversations about what screens are, why we use them, and that they are tools rather than the centre of life.

## Principles that apply at every age

**Transitions matter more than time.** Abrupt endings cause distress. A five-minute warning before screen time ends makes the transition far easier.

**Physical play is not negotiable.** Whatever screen time your child has, ensure it does not displace outdoor play, physical movement, and hands-on creative play.

**Your own relationship with screens matters.** Children learn from observation. Putting your own phone down during family time, and explaining why, is one of the most effective things you can do.

**There is no perfect.** Occasional extra screen time during a sick day, a long journey, or a stressful week is not harmful. Patterns over months matter; individual days do not.

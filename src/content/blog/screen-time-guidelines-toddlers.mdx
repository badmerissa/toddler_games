Screen time advice for parents of toddlers can feel overwhelming — and contradictory. One article says any screen time before age two is harmful; another says interactive digital play is beneficial. So what does the evidence actually say?

## What the major health organisations recommend

**World Health Organization (WHO)**

The WHO advises that children under 1 year should have no screen time at all (other than video calling with family). For children aged 1–2, it recommends avoiding sedentary screen time, while for ages 3–4 it suggests limiting it to one hour per day.

**American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)**

The AAP distinguishes between *passive* and *interactive* screen use. For children under 18 months, they recommend avoiding screen media other than video chats. Between 18 and 24 months, they suggest that if parents choose to introduce digital media, they should choose high-quality content and watch it *with* their child. For ages 2–5, the recommendation is up to one hour per day of high-quality programming.

**NHS (UK)**

The NHS does not set a specific hour limit for children over 2, but recommends that screen time should not replace sleep, physical activity, or face-to-face interaction. They emphasise *what* your child does online and *how* you engage with them, rather than a strict time limit.

## Passive vs. interactive: a crucial distinction

Not all screen time is created equal. The research increasingly points to a meaningful difference between:

- **Passive consumption** — watching videos, auto-playing content
- **Interactive engagement** — colouring apps, problem-solving games, video calling family

Several studies suggest that interactive digital play can support cognitive development, fine motor skills, and even language acquisition when it is age-appropriate and accompanied by adult engagement.

## The role of co-engagement

One of the strongest predictors of whether screen time benefits or harms young children is whether a parent or caregiver is present and engaged. Talking through what is happening on screen, asking questions, and connecting digital experiences to real-world objects dramatically increases the educational value of any activity.

## Practical principles for parents

1. **Prioritise interaction over consumption.** Choose apps that respond to your child's input over those that play automatically.
2. **Watch with your child.** Even five minutes of shared screen time is more valuable than 30 minutes alone.
3. **Set consistent limits.** Children thrive on routine. A predictable end to screen time prevents tantrums better than arbitrary cut-offs.
4. **Screen-free times and spaces.** Mealtimes, the hour before bed, and bedrooms are sensible defaults.
5. **Trust your instincts.** If your child becomes irritable, struggles to disengage, or is substituting screens for physical play and sleep, it is worth reassessing.

## The bottom line

The research does not support the idea that any screen time before age two causes permanent harm. Context, content, and co-engagement matter far more than the clock. The guidelines are a starting point, not a verdict on your parenting.
